📉 Why did the 2024 Impact Factors of Nature and Science drop?
A quick look at the trend shows a steep rise during 2021–2022 and a noticeable fall in 2024. 🔍 Here’s why: 1. The COVID-19 citation surge has ended. Pandemic-era papers drew an unprecedented number of citations in 2020–2021, inflating impact factors. Those highly cited papers have now aged out of the 2-year citation window used for calculation. 2. Clarivate changed the game. The new rule includes early-access articles in both the numerator and the denominator. Since journals like Nature and Science publish many early-access pieces, their article counts went up—diluting the impact factor. ⚠️ Important takeaway: The drop doesn’t reflect declining quality—just evolving metrics and the return to non-pandemic citation behavior.

🎓 A PhD Is Not Enough (4): The Shortcut to the "Iron Rice Bowl" — Academic Tenure vs. Research Careers
The Shortcut to the "Iron Rice Bowl" Completing your PhD is a monumental achievement, but it’s not the end—it’s the start of a crucial career crossroads. As you leave the comfort of student life, you’re faced with a fundamental choice: 🔹 Stay in academia? 🔹 Pursue research in industry or government? Both paths profoundly impact your career, quality of life, and even personal relationships. The Allure of Tenure: The ‘Iron Rice Bowl’ Tenured faculty positions at universities were designed to protect academic freedom and provide job security. The advantages are real: Job Security: Tenure is one of the last true ‘iron rice bowls’—your job is secure unless your department shuts down. Research Freedom: Choose your topics, collaborators, and pace. Respect & Impact: Influence generations of students and gain recognition in your field and beyond. Flexible Earning: Supplement your income with consulting, textbooks, or even your own company. Sabbaticals: Paid time off to recharge or explore new fields. The Harsh Realities Behind the Podium But professorship is far from an easy ride: Multi-role Overload: Beyond research, you teach, advise, fundraise, recruit, and serve on endless committees. Heavy Workload: Prepping new courses, mentoring students, and departmental duties consume private time. Financial Constraints: Many professors earn only a 9-month salary. Without grants or side gigs, finances are tight. Internal Politics: Academic collaboration is often undermined by competition within departments. High Entry Bar: The typical path—Assistant Professor, then up—is filled with stress, uncertainty, and fewer benefits than you’d expect. Assistant Professor: All the Work, Few of the Perks Starting as an Assistant Professor often means: Low Pay, High Pressure: Lower salary, tougher grant applications, and high expectations. Uncertain Stability: “Up or out” policies mean you must secure promotion within 6–7 years or leave. Intense Competition: Prestigious institutions offer less pay but attract more candidates. Industry & Government Labs: A Different Road Research positions outside academia offer: Higher Starting Salaries: Especially early in your career. Collaborative Environment: Strong management, shared resources, and cross-disciplinary teamwork. Merit-Based Growth: Performance, not politics, drives recognition. More Personal Time: Focus on research—no teaching or endless committee meetings. But beware: Limited Academic Freedom: Shifting priorities or management can redirect your work or claim credit. Publishing Restrictions: Confidential work may limit publications and academic reputation. Fewer Helpers: Less access to postdocs and grad students for large-scale projects. Key Questions Before Returning to Academia: Is your industry/government research academically impactful? Can you publish and build your reputation? If you value freedom, impact, and collaboration, weigh these choices carefully. Your career—and life—will thank you. Reference: Feibelman, P. J. (2011). A PhD Is Not Enough!: A Guide to Survival in Science.
